### What They Build

Factory 4 produces **"parts, assemblies, and finished products"** for submarine
construction. No specific component types (valves, piping, hull sections,
propulsion elements, etc.) have been publicly disclosed.

Manufacturing processes include:
- CNC machining
- Welding
- Fabrication
- Assembly
- Inspection and testing

Target: **~80% automation rate** across all processes.

### Which Submarine Programs

| Program | Class | Prime Shipbuilder |
|---|---|---|
| Columbia-class SSBN | Ballistic missile submarine | GD Electric Boat (lead), HII Newport News (modules) |
| Virginia-class SSN | Attack submarine | GD Electric Boat (lead), HII Newport News (modules) |

### Which Shipyards Receive Parts

Not publicly confirmed. However, the stated purpose is to **"free up submarine
shipyards in Rhode Island, Connecticut and Virginia"** to focus on module
production -- pointing to:

- **General Dynamics Electric Boat** (Groton, CT and Quonset Point, RI)
- **HII Newport News Shipbuilding** (Newport News, VA)

Hadrian's position is as a **supplier to the submarine shipyards**, producing
components and assemblies that the shipyards currently manufacture in-house or
source from dozens of smaller vendors. The company is not a shipyard competitor.

### Expansion Plans

## Maritime Industrial Base Context

### The Problem Hadrian Is Addressing

The submarine industrial base faces a critical capacity bottleneck:
- The Navy needs **1 Columbia-class SSBN + 2.33 Virginia-class SSN per year**
- Current production rate is well below target
- Skilled welders and machinists are scarce (workforce shortages across all shipyards)
- HII Newport News is **outsourcing 2M+ hours** of submarine work in 2026 (30% increase from 2025), distributing work across 23 smaller shipyards and manufacturing centers

Hadrian's pitch: automate the "technically challenging steps" that currently
require scarce skilled labor, achieve 80% automation, and train new workers
in 30 days instead of years. This theoretically allows submarine shipyards
to focus their skilled workforce on final module assembly and integration.

### Where Hadrian Fits in the Supplier Tier

```
Tier 0 (Program Primes)
  └── GD Electric Boat (Columbia lead, Virginia lead)
  └── HII Newport News (Columbia modules, Virginia modules)

Tier 1 (Major Subsystem / Component Suppliers)
  └── Hadrian Automation [NEW ENTRANT]  ← Factory 4 (submarine components)
  └── BWX Technologies (naval nuclear reactors)
  └── Northrop Grumman (submarine electronics, sensors)
  └── L3Harris (submarine communications, EW)
  └── DRS Naval Power Systems (power electronics)
  └── Fairbanks Morse (diesel generators)
  └── Etc.

Tier 2+ (Parts, Materials, Specialty)
  └── Hundreds of small/medium suppliers
```

Hadrian is positioning as a **Tier 1 supplier** that consolidates work currently
spread across dozens of Tier 2/3 vendors into a single automated factory. The
company's stated model is "raw material in, test-ready hardware out" -- replacing
the fragmented multi-vendor supply chain with vertically integrated automated
production.

## Risk Factors & Open Questions

1. **$3M revenue vs. $2.4B commitment.** Hadrian's 2024 revenue was ~$3M. The
   Navy partnership represents an ~800x scale-up. Factory 4 is a 2.2M sq ft
   facility that needs to reach full-rate production within 24 months.

2. **No FPDS trail.** Unlike established defense suppliers with decades of
   contract history, Hadrian has zero federal contract records. The company
   has operated as a commercial supplier to primes (Anduril, SpaceX) rather
   than as a direct government contractor.

3. **Component specifics undisclosed.** No public information identifies which
   specific submarine components Factory 4 will produce. "Parts, assemblies,
   and finished products" is deliberately vague. The complexity gap between
   simple machined parts and submarine-grade welded assemblies is enormous.

4. **Shipyard customer not confirmed.** While Electric Boat and HII Newport
   News are the only two submarine shipyards, Hadrian hasn't publicly named
   which one(s) will receive Factory 4 output.

5. **Performance-based structure.** The Navy funds "only as factory demonstrates
   results" -- this de-risks the government side but means Hadrian's $1.5B
   private capital is at risk if production targets aren't met.

6. **Novel contract vehicle.** The "novel transaction" structure may mean this
   doesn't follow standard FPDS/FAR reporting, making ongoing tracking difficult
   through public procurement databases.
