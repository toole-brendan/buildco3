# US Coast Guard Taxonomy of Work on In-Service Cutters and Boats

## Source: Naval Engineering Manual (NEM), COMDTINST M9000.6G (2004)

---

## Key Finding: The Coast Guard Uses a Three-Part Typology, Not "MRO"

The Coast Guard does not use "MRO" as a unified category. Instead, the NEM defines **three types of maintenance** that encompass all work on in-service vessels. Notably, the CG treats configuration changes ("alterative maintenance") as a **type of maintenance** rather than a separate program — a structural difference from the Navy, which separates maintenance and modernization into distinct programs with distinct budgets.

---

## I. THREE TYPES OF MAINTENANCE

### A. Corrective Maintenance (Restore After Failure)

> "Corrective Maintenance is the performance of scheduled and unscheduled tasks designed to restore equipment after failure has occurred."
> — Chapter 081, Section E, p. ~100

Tracked via CASREPs (Casualty Reports), CSMPs (Current Ship's Maintenance Projects), and CMAs (Corrective Maintenance Actions).

Formal definition from Chapter 076:
> "Maintenance required to restore operation of systems or equipment to design parameters when functional failure has occurred."
> — Chapter 076, p. ~46

### B. Preventive Maintenance (Prevent Failure Before It Occurs)

> "Preventive maintenance outlines minimum maintenance requirements and procedures for HM&E, damage control, electronic and ordnance equipment aboard cutters and standard boats. Procedures are prescribed for inspecting, cleaning, and reconditioning machinery and equipment, to counter the effects of age and environment."
> — Chapter 081, Section E, p. ~101

Formal definition from Chapter 076:
> "Maintenance actions taken to minimize conditions that cause unacceptable degradation of functions, prior to the occurrence of actual failure. This includes time-directed, condition-directed, failure finding, servicing and lubrication tasks."
> — Chapter 076, p. ~46

Tracked via Class Maintenance Plan (CMP) and Planned Maintenance System (PMS).

#### Five Sub-Types of Preventive Maintenance

| Sub-Type | Abbreviation | Description |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Condition-Directed** | CD | Life renewal based on measured condition vs. standard. Identifies impending failures before they occur. |
| **Time-Directed** | TD | Life renewal regardless of condition, based on known age-failure relationship. |
| **Failure-Finding** | FF | Identify hidden failures not evident during normal operation, often concerning safety/protective devices. |
| **Servicing & Lubrication** | — | Manufacturer-required tasks to maintain equipment in running order. |
| **Condition-Based Maintenance** | CBM | Technology-supported maintenance using thermographic imaging, vibration monitoring, bore scope inspection, flow measurement, ultrasonic inspection, and laser alignment. |

**Source:** Chapter 076, Section F.4, pp. ~50-52; Section G, pp. ~52-55

### C. Alterative Maintenance (Change Configuration)

> "Alterative maintenance improves system performance by changing configuration to meet environmental, safety, operational, or economic requirements."
> — Chapter 081, Section E, p. ~101

Formal definition from Chapter 076:
> "Changes to equipment or systems that change the design or design capabilities to improve inherent reliability or functionality."
> — Chapter 076, p. ~46

Tracked via Engineering Changes (Chapter 041). This is the Coast Guard's equivalent of what the Navy calls "Modernization."

## II. THREE LEVELS OF MAINTENANCE (Who Does the Work)

The CG uses **"tri-level maintenance"**:

| Level | Performer | Scope |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Organizational** | Unit's assigned crew (or cutter-funded commercial contract / Maintenance Augmentation Team) | First-echelon maintenance performed by the ship's company |
| **Intermediate** | Outside assistance — industrial activities, contracted support, Maintenance Augmentation Teams (MATs), ASSIST teams | More sophisticated maintenance that requires outside skills/equipment but is not a major rebuild |
| **Depot** | Commercial or industrial shipyard activity, typically during drydock or dockside availability | Major overhaul or complete rebuild of parts, assemblies, subassemblies, and end-items, including manufacture of parts, modifications, testing, and reclamation |

**Source:** Chapter 081, Section C, p. ~99

### Support Teams

- **MAT (Maintenance Augmentation Teams)**: NESU-assigned crews for intermediate-level maintenance, particularly on chartered cutters. (Chapter 088, p. ~135)
- **ASSIST (Advanced Ship System Instruction and Support Teams)**: MLC-assigned highly skilled technicians for inspections, mobile technical support, instruction in organizational maintenance, and complex casualty repairs. (Chapter 088, p. ~135)

---

## III. ENGINEERING CHANGES (Alterative Maintenance Detail)

Engineering Changes are the CG's mechanism for all modifications/alterations to in-service vessels. The old nomenclature was **"Ship and Boat Alteration" (ShipAlt/BoatAlt)** — serial numbers carried forward from that system.

**Source:** Chapter 041, p. ~31

### A. Two Categories by Scope

| Category | Scope |
|----------|-------|
| **Platform Engineering Change** | Modifications that change mission characteristics, stability, hull structure, space allocations, watertight integrity, or compartmentation |
| **System Engineering Change** | Changes to systems/components documented on APLs, changes to software/fluid/paint systems, or form/fit/function changes |

**Source:** Chapter 041, p. ~24

### B. Three Classes by Urgency

| Class | Urgency | Timeline |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Class A** | Utmost importance — impairs service, safety, or health. "Equivalent to urgent repairs." | Must be done within one availability cycle |
| **Class B** | Less urgent but important for service improvement, health/comfort, efficiency | Within two availability cycles |
| **Class C** | Contingent on a specific event (e.g., funding receipt, item renewal) | Once triggered, must be completed within two years |

**Source:** Chapter 041, p. ~31

### C. Three Tiers by Cost

| Tier | Funding Source | Threshold |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| **Low Cost** | Unit AFC-30 | Total cost ≤ MLC CSMP threshold |
| **Medium Cost** | MLC AFC-4X | Cost > threshold but ≤ 5% of class SSL |
| **High Cost** | Program Manager (3X/4X/AC&I) | Cost > 5% of class SSL |

**Source:** Chapter 041, p. ~30

---

## IV. RE-CAPITALIZATION ("World of Work")

Re-capitalization is the CG's term for **major equipment replacement/upgrade Engineering Changes** funded from a dedicated AFC-45 account:

> "The Naval Engineering community developed a re-capitalization process. This process was designed to appropriately fund Engineering Changes in order to produce the greatest return on investment, while maximizing equipment standardization, minimizing life cycle logistics support costs."
> — Chapter 081, Section I, p. ~107

Re-cap projects follow the full Engineering Change process (concept, validation, development, deployment) and are typically completed within one drydock cycle.

**Source:** Chapter 081, Section I, pp. ~107-109

## V. SERVICE LIFE EXTENSION PROGRAMS (Major Non-MRO Work)

Beyond routine maintenance and standard engineering changes, the CG has historically executed **major programs** to extend the service life of entire cutter classes. These represent the largest-scale work done on existing vessels:

| Program | Abbreviation | Cutter Class | Year | Description |
|---------|-------------|-------------|------|-------------|
| Fleet Rehabilitation and Modernization | **FRAM** | 378' WHEC | 1989 | Comprehensive modernization |
| Reactivation/Improvement Program | **RIP** | 400' WAGB | — | Reactivation + improvement |
| Major Maintenance Availability | **MMA** | 210' WMEC | 1987 | Major depot maintenance |
| Service Life Extension Program | **SLEP** | 180' A Class | 1984 | Extend design service life |
| Renovation | **REN** | 180' A Class | 1998 | Major renovation |
| Major Renovation | **MAJ REN** | 180' C Class WLB | 1978 | Comprehensive renovation |

These programs are referenced collectively in Chapter 091:
> "Cutters, which undergo a major overhaul such as SLEP, MAJOR REN, MMA, RIP, or FRAM, shall have a stability test."
> — Chapter 091, p. ~190

ShipAlts can also extend service life: "15 year design Service Life extended to 25 years on 110' WPBs with ShipAlt 110-A-021." (Chapter 091, p. ~184)

**Source:** Chapter 091, Table 091-2, pp. ~182-184

## VI. SERVICE LIFE EVALUATION (Renovate vs. Replace Decisions)

The **SSMEB (Ship Structure and Machinery Evaluation Board)** determines remaining service life and informs renovate-versus-replace decisions:

> "Remaining Service Life shall be defined as the time period during which no 'major expenditures' will be required for hull and structural repairs or modernizations, nor for machinery or system modernizations based solely on the cutter's/boat's capability to meet existing mission requirements."
> — Chapter 091, p. ~171

| Board | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| **SSMEB** | Full evaluation (structure + machinery + equipment) |
| **MEB** (Machinery Evaluation Board) | Machinery and systems only |
| **SSEB** (Ship Structure Evaluation Board) | Structure only |

**Source:** Chapter 091, pp. ~171-176

## VII. AVAILABILITY TYPES (Scheduling Framework)

| Type | Abbreviation | Description | Typical Interval | Duration |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|----------|
| **Drydock** | DD | Major availability, vessel hauled out | 2-4 years | 5-12 weeks |
| **Dockside** | DS | Vessel remains waterborne | 1-3 per DD cycle | 4-12 weeks |
| **Maintenance & Repair Days** | M&R Days | Routine time not part of DD/DS | 6.6-17.9 weeks/year | Ongoing |
| **Emergency / CASREP** | — | Urgent response to equipment failure | As needed | Variable |

> "Required Maintenance Days Per Year reflect the average number of days historically required in a year to accomplish preventive maintenance (PMS), corrective maintenance (CSMPs, CASREPs), and alterative maintenance (Engineering Changes)."
> — Chapter 081, Section N, p. ~110

**Source:** Chapter 081, Section N, Table 081-2, pp. ~110-112

## VIII. FUNDING STRUCTURE

| Account | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **AFC-30** (Unit funds) | Organizational maintenance, PMS, repairs, consumables, boat overhauls |
| **AFC-42** | Electronic equipment maintenance |
| **AFC-45 SSL** | Intermediate/depot maintenance managed by MLCs |
| **AFC-45 Re-capitalization** | Major Engineering Changes ("World of Work") |
| **AFC-45 ELC Base** | Overhead and support programs |
| **AFC-45 Standard Boats** | Emergency casualty repairs (storm, grounding, fire, collision) |
| **AC&I** (Acquisition, Construction & Improvement) | High-cost Engineering Changes (>5% of class SSL) |

> AFC-45 funds cover "the maintenance, repair, and alteration of all Cutters and Boats."
> — Chapter 081, p. ~105

**Source:** Chapter 081, Sections H-K, pp. ~105-109

## IX. COMPLETE WORK TAXONOMY SUMMARY

```
WORK ON IN-SERVICE COAST GUARD CUTTERS AND BOATS
│
├── CORRECTIVE MAINTENANCE (restore after failure)
│   ├── Tracked via CASREPs, CSMPs, CMAs
│   └── Ranges from minor repairs to major casualty restoration
│
├── PREVENTIVE MAINTENANCE (prevent failure)
│   ├── Condition-Directed (CD) — measured condition vs. standard
│   ├── Time-Directed (TD) — age-based, regardless of condition
│   ├── Failure-Finding (FF) — detect hidden failures
│   ├── Servicing & Lubrication — manufacturer requirements
│   └── Condition-Based Maintenance (CBM) — sensor/technology supported
│
├── ALTERATIVE MAINTENANCE (change configuration)
│   ├── Engineering Changes
│   │   ├── By Scope: Platform EC vs. System EC
│   │   ├── By Urgency: Class A (urgent) / B (important) / C (contingent)
│   │   └── By Cost: Low (unit) / Medium (MLC) / High (Program Manager/AC&I)
│   ├── Re-capitalization ("World of Work") — major equipment replacement
│   └── Service Life Extension Programs (SLEP, FRAM, MMA, RIP, REN, MAJ REN)
│
├── By Maintenance Level (Who Does It)
│   ├── Organizational (ship's crew)
│   ├── Intermediate (MATs, ASSIST teams, contractors)
│   └── Depot (shipyard, drydock/dockside)
│
├── By Availability Type (When It Happens)
│   ├── Drydock (DD) — 2-4 year intervals, 5-12 weeks
│   ├── Dockside (DS) — 1-3 per DD cycle, 4-12 weeks
│   ├── M&R Days — routine, 6.6-17.9 weeks/year
│   └── Emergency/CASREP — as needed
│
├── By Funding Source
│   ├── AFC-30 (unit/organizational)
│   ├── AFC-42 (electronics)
│   ├── AFC-45 SSL (intermediate/depot)
│   ├── AFC-45 Re-cap (engineering changes)
│   └── AC&I (high-cost modifications)
│
└── By Contracting Mechanism (Depot Work)
    ├── SFLC Standard Specifications (modular spec library)
    ├── Definite CLINs (planned baseline work)
    ├── Option CLINs (contingent on inspection findings)
    ├── Condition-Found Reports (growth work authorization)
    ├── Critical Inspection Reports (progress gates)
    └── Testing: Pre-work → Post-repair → Dock/Sea Trials
```

## X. DEPOT WORK SPECIFICATION AND CONTRACTING FRAMEWORK

While the NEM defines *what types of work exist* and *who performs them*, the **SFLC Standard Specification** system defines *how depot-level work gets specified, contracted, and executed* at commercial shipyards. This is the operational layer where CG maintenance dollars flow to private industry.

### A. SFLC Standard Specifications

The Surface Forces Logistics Center (SFLC) maintains a library of numbered standard specifications that govern all contracted ship repair work. Each availability contract is built from these modular specifications:

| Spec Number | Subject |
|-------------|---------|
| **0000** | General Requirements — master boilerplate for all ship repair availabilities |
| **0342** | Docking and Undocking |
| **0450** | Electrical Power for Contractor's Tools and Equipment |
| **0740** | Welding and Allied Processes / Nondestructive Examination (NDE) |
| **5000** | Hydraulic System Cleanliness |
| **6310** | Preservation of Ship Structures (coatings, surface preparation, paint systems) |
| **6341** | Deck Covering Systems |
| **8635** | Shore Power |

> SFLC Std Spec 0000 "invokes general requirements for conducting ship repair availabilities for Coast Guard vessels."
> — SFLC Std Spec 0000, Section 1.1, p. 1

The specification system is hierarchical. SFLC Std Spec 0000 is always included and establishes the order of precedence for all contract references:

1. Text of the specification work items
2. Text of the General Requirements specifications
3. Coast Guard Drawings
4. Coast Guard Technical Publications
5. Commercial Drawings
6. Commercial Technical Manuals
7. Coast Guard Standards and Instructions
8. Commercial or Industrial Standards
9. Commercial Practices
10. Federal and Military Specifications and Standards

**Source:** SFLC Std Spec 0000, Section 3.2.1.1, p. 8

### B. Definite and Option Work Items

Individual work items within an availability contract are organized as **Contract Line Item Numbers (CLINs)** and classified into two categories:

| Category | Designation | Description |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Definite** | D | Planned work — the baseline scope of the availability, known before contract award |
| **Option** | O | Contingent work — priced but not authorized until a specific condition is confirmed during execution |

Example from the specification: "CLIN 19-D, Propeller Shaft Remove, Inspect, and Reinstall" (definite — always performed) vs. "CLIN 25-O, Propeller Shaft Covering Renew" (option — authorized only if inspection reveals the covering needs renewal).

This Definite/Option structure is the contracting mechanism through which the NEM's maintenance types manifest in practice:
- **Preventive maintenance** tasks (inspections, condition assessments) are typically Definite items
- **Corrective maintenance** tasks (repairs discovered during inspection) are typically Option items or new work authorized via the Condition-Found Report process (see below)

**Source:** SFLC Std Spec 0000, Section 3.2.6.1.4.2, p. 13

### C. Condition-Found Reports (CFRs) — The Growth Work Mechanism

The **Condition-Found Report (CFR)** is the formal process by which unplanned work is identified, evaluated, and authorized during an availability. It is the operational bridge between preventive maintenance (the inspection that discovers a problem) and corrective maintenance (the repair that follows).

> "CFR: Condition-Found Report. Report submitted to the Contracting Officer's Representative, either in written or electronic format, describing the condition(s) found while performing a task specified in the work item, such as an inspection."
> — SFLC Std Spec 0000, Acronyms and Term Definitions, p. 2

When a contractor discovers an abnormal condition during the course of a work item, the CFR must include:

1. The CLIN and title to which the condition relates
2. A clear description of the condition found (frame numbers, part numbers, materials, dimensions)
3. A proposed or recommended repair, with materials and dimensions
4. PASS/FAIL determination for each measurement against allowable tolerances
5. Whether Coast Guard action is required, with response deadline
6. Schedule impact — how many additional days past the scheduled completion date the added work will require

> "Conditions are discovered by the Contractor which are considered abnormal for reasons of safety, expected reliability, health, or habitability; these conditions must be brought to the attention of the Coast Guard using a CFR. Details provided by the Contractor in a CFR are important because the CFR may result in a contract change."
> — SFLC Std Spec 0000, Section 3.2.6.1.4.2 (Note), p. 13

CFRs for routine inspections must be submitted within 24 hours of completing the inspection. CFRs for unexpected conditions must be submitted within 24 hours of discovery.

**Source:** SFLC Std Spec 0000, Sections 3.2.6.1.4.1–3.2.6.1.4.2, pp. 13–14

### D. Critical Inspection Reports (CIRs)

Distinct from CFRs, **Critical Inspection Reports (CIRs)** are submitted at designated percentage-of-completion milestones during the availability, describing conditions found during specified inspection tasks. CIRs serve as formal progress gates.

> "CIR: Critical Inspection Report. Report submitted to the COR within designated percent of the availability contract period, either in written or electronic format, describing the condition(s) found while performing a task specified in the work item, such as an inspection."
> — SFLC Std Spec 0000, Acronyms and Term Definitions, p. 2

**Source:** SFLC Std Spec 0000, p. 2

### E. Testing Framework

The specification mandates a three-stage testing sequence for all work:

| Stage | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Pre-work** | Initial Test | Witness operational pre-test of all items to be disturbed, repaired, or altered — establishes baseline condition |
| **Post-work** | Post-Repair Test | Thoroughly test and prove all items disturbed, repaired, altered, or installed — must be in same or better condition than pre-work |
| **Acceptance** | Dock and Sea Trials | System-level trials to validate work performed during the contract; scheduled within the planning document |

> "All machinery, electronics and electrical equipment shall be in the same or better condition as the pre-work checks."
> — SFLC Std Spec 0000, Section 3.3.5.1.1, p. 22

**Source:** SFLC Std Spec 0000, Sections 3.3.13.1–3.3.13.2, pp. 24–25

## XI. KEY STRUCTURAL DIFFERENCE FROM THE US NAVY

| Dimension | US Navy | US Coast Guard |
|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **Top-level split** | Maintenance and Modernization are **separate programs** with separate budgets and governance | All work is classified under **three types of maintenance** (corrective, preventive, alterative) — modernization is subsumed as "alterative maintenance" |
| **Modification terminology** | Ship Changes (Program Changes + Fleet Changes); SHIPALTs, MACHALTs, ORDALTs, etc. | Engineering Changes (Platform EC + System EC); historically ShipAlts/BoatAlts |
| **Availability complexity** | 25+ named CNO-scheduled availability types (SRA, DSRA, PMA, COH, RCOH, etc.) | Three availability windows: Drydock, Dockside, M&R Days |
| **Major life extension** | Depot Modernization Period, RCOH, overhaul types | Named programs per class: SLEP, FRAM, MMA, RIP, REN, MAJ REN |
| **Maintenance strategy** | EOC, IMP, PM, PROG (class-level strategies) | CMP/PMS-driven; RCM as the analytical framework |
